Variance Reduction in Monte Carlo Simulation

Enhancing Monte Carlo efficiency through Antithetic Variates and Control Variates methods.

Antithetic Variates Method

The Antithetic Variates method is a variance reduction technique that leverages negatively correlated random variables to improve Monte Carlo efficiency. Instead of generating independent random samples, we generate pairs of positively and negatively correlated values.

This approach helps to reduce fluctuations in simulation results, leading to more accurate estimates with fewer simulation runs. It is particularly effective in pricing financial derivatives and risk assessment models.

Control Variates Method

The Control Variates method improves Monte Carlo accuracy by using known expected values of correlated variables to adjust simulation estimates.

  • Reduces simulation error by leveraging known statistical properties.
  • Uses a correlated control variable to adjust the primary estimate.
  • Commonly applied in financial option pricing and risk management.

By incorporating auxiliary information, the Control Variates method ensures faster convergence of Monte Carlo estimations with reduced computational effort.

Discretization of Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM) SDE

Monte Carlo simulations for stochastic differential equations (SDEs) require numerical discretization methods. The most common approaches are the Euler-Maruyama, Euler Implicit, and Milstein schemes.

Monte Carlo Simulation of GBM

The Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM) is widely used in financial modeling, particularly for asset prices. It follows the stochastic differential equation:

$$ dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma S_t dW_t $$

where \( S_t \) is the asset price, \( \mu \) is the drift, \( \sigma \) is the volatility, and \( W_t \) is a Wiener process.

Monte Carlo methods approximate solutions by simulating many random paths of \( S_t \) using discrete time steps.

Numerical Discretization Methods

  • Euler-Maruyama Scheme: The simplest method, using a first-order approximation:

    $$ S_{t+\Delta t} = S_t + \mu S_t \Delta t + \sigma S_t \sqrt{\Delta t} \, Z_t $$

    where \( Z_t \sim N(0,1) \).
  • Milstein Scheme: An improved version that includes an extra correction term:

    $$ S_{t+\Delta t} = S_t + \mu S_t \Delta t + \sigma S_t \sqrt{\Delta t} \, Z_t + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 S_t (\Delta t Z_t^2 - \Delta t) $$

  • Euler Implicit Scheme: An implicit scheme that offers improved stability:

    $$ S_{t+\Delta t} = \frac{S_t + \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t} Z_t}{1 - \mu \Delta t} $$

    This method requires solving a nonlinear equation at each time step, ensuring better stability in stiff equations.
  • Monte Carlo Convergence: The Milstein scheme reduces discretization error and improves convergence compared to Euler-Maruyama.

These numerical methods allow efficient simulation of asset prices under stochastic processes, essential for option pricing and risk management.

Heston Stochastic Volatility Model

The Heston model is a widely used stochastic volatility model in financial mathematics.

Understanding the Heston Model

The Heston model is a stochastic volatility model used to describe the evolution of asset prices. It extends the Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM) by allowing volatility to be stochastic.

It is defined by the system of stochastic differential equations (SDEs):

$$ dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sqrt{V_t} S_t dW_t^S $$

$$ dV_t = \kappa (\theta - V_t) dt + \sigma \sqrt{V_t} dW_t^V $$

where:

  • \( S_t \) = asset price
  • \( V_t \) = variance (volatility squared)
  • \( \mu \) = risk-free rate
  • \( \kappa \) = mean-reversion speed
  • \( \theta \) = long-term variance
  • \( \sigma \) = volatility of variance
  • \( W_t^S \) and \( W_t^V \) = correlated Wiener processes with correlation \( \rho \)

The correlation between \( W_t^S \) and \( W_t^V \) is given by:

$$ dW_t^S dW_t^V = \rho dt $$

Key Features & Applications

  • Mean-reverting variance: The variance \( V_t \) tends to revert to \( \theta \).
  • Explains volatility smile/skew: Unlike the Black-Scholes model, the Heston model captures market-implied volatility structures.
  • Used in option pricing: The model is widely used for pricing derivatives.

The model is solved using numerical methods like Monte Carlo simulation, Fourier transform methods, and finite difference schemes.

Volatility Analysis using the Heston Model

Exploring volatility smile and surface plots derived from the Heston stochastic volatility model.

Understanding Volatility Smile

The volatility smile is a curve that represents the implied volatility of options across different strike prices. The Heston model captures this phenomenon, unlike the Black-Scholes model, which assumes constant volatility.

Key Observations:

  • Out-of-the-money (OTM) and in-the-money (ITM) options tend to have higher implied volatilities than at-the-money (ATM) options.
  • Market impact: Volatility smiles are commonly observed in equity, FX, and commodity options.
  • Heston’s stochastic volatility model provides a more realistic volatility structure by allowing variance to change over time.

Volatility Surface Visualization

The volatility surface extends the volatility smile concept by incorporating both strike price and time to maturity.

The Heston model generates a 3D volatility surface that captures the dynamics of implied volatility over time.

Key Features:

  • Skew and curvature: Helps traders and risk managers assess mispricing in options markets.
  • Used in exotic option pricing: Important for pricing barrier options, volatility derivatives, and other structured products.
